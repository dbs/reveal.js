Linked bibliographic data

  • From bibliographic records to linked bibliographic data:
    • Dublin Core Metadata Terms (dcterms) - 2003-
    • Bibliographic Ontology (bibo) - 2008-2009
    • FRBR-Aligned Bibliographic Ontology (fabio) - 2010-
    • Bibliographic Framework (BIBFRAME) - 2012-
    • Resource Description Access (RDA) - 2014-
dcterms is used extensively, both as the basis of other vocabularies, and as a lightweight vocabulary for institutional repositories and other special collections.

While bibo and fabio were developed relatively early, and have influenced other vocabularies such as schema.org, they have found low adoption rates.

BIBFRAME is an initiative led by the Library of Congress to provide a successor for the 50-year old MARC format for bibliographic interchange. The second iteration of BIBFRAME in 2016 saw an international set of libraries including Harvard, Stanford, Cornell, and the University of Alberta commit to adopting BIBFRAME as their primary cataloguing format.

RDA is an effort to produce a vocabulary aligned with FRBR (now IFLA LRM) that targets all cultural heritage institutions.

Data quality: why?

  • Standards are not sufficient, nor standard (Zhu et al, 2016)
  • Bibliographic data: history of collaborative efforts and challenges
  • Linked data: broad scope, sophisticated models, but limited results
  • Linked bibliographic data: early days, wide open

Seminal definition of data quality

data that are fit for use by data consumers Wang & Strong (1996)
  • "data consumers" means context is critical
  • Context for this work was MIS with defence systems
This definition is cited by most of the articles in the LIS and computer science fields.

Gaps and opportunities

Generalizability

  • Linked data: yes, but specificity is lacking
  • Bibliographic literature: no
    • Small scale
    • Dublin Core
    • Context bound

Reproducibility

  • External validity threat: history vs. open datasets
  • Lack of access to implementations, in-house datasets

Automation: breadth vs. depth

  • Billions of records: mostly syntax
  • Handfuls of records: rich semantics
Context makes this hard!

David & Thomas (2015) analyzed logs to determine 4 key access points (title, author, year, subject), analyzed 9,800 records; it would take 208 person-days to check all of the records in their platform

Stvilia et al. (2007) constructed a meticulous framework, then tested only a subset of it against Dublin Core records

  • Linked bibliographic data has more specific context
  • ∴ "fitness for use" can be more meaningfully determined
  • and an automated method may made be more useful

Research questions

  1. How should we conceive of quality in the domain of linked bibliographic data?
  2. How can we meaningfully assess the quality of linked bibliographic data?
  3. How can we assess the quality of linked bibliographic data at scale?

Appendices

Proposal

  1. Synthesize a conceptual model for quality assessment of linked bibliographic data
  2. Compile a gold standard dataset of linked bibliographic data using the BIBFRAME vocabulary (Aalberg et al., 2018; Decourselle, 2016)
  3. Implement the conceptual model metrics in an automated assessment tool
  1. Using grounded theory analysis as described by Jabberdeen (2009) and Charmaz (2014)
  2. Coordinating BIBFRAME experts to compile and evaluate the quality of the dataset
  3. Written in Python, using the dataset as a test bed

Significance

  • Constructivist grounded theory is a new approach to model development for data quality
  • Gold standard dataset will ease the efforts of other researchers, in data quality and other inquiries
  • Assessment tool should enable libraries to evaluate and improve their linked bibliographic data early
