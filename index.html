Chasing citations

  • Bibliometrics
  • Impact factor
  • h-index

Impact factor = average # of times each article in the journal has been cited

h-index is the number of articles h for a given author which have been cited h or more times

All being gamed (self-citations, both of authors and journals, etc)

Venue: Books (science)

  • Usually edited volumes: e.g. 8,500 Springer "life sciences" titles
  • Editor may get a small stipend
  • Chapter authors may get a discount
  • Visibility may not be high

Venue: Conferences

  • "Presented a paper at a conference"
  • Can include full peer review
  • Results collected in proceedings
  • Excellent networking opportunities
  • Also subject to predatory behaviour

Venue: Journals

  • Peer-reviewed high-impact journals: "gold standard"
  • But it's complicated

Venue: Preprint Archives

"immediate dissemination and open access to scholarly articles" (arXiv.org)

  • May include commenting, open reviews, etc.
  • Often a precursor to traditional journal, with benefit of early, broad feedback
  • Preprints can be (and are) cited

"The one affiliated with Laurentian University" works as long as you are a student or employed there; but researchers change institutions and contact information. Establish your personal profiles.

Researcher profiles

  • ORCID (Open, non-profit)
  • ResearcherID (Web of Science)
  • Google Scholar (Google)

ORCID was developed based on ResearcherID's technology, but is open and non-profit. You can link your ResearcherID and ORCID profiles.

Google Scholar is a heavily-used academic search engine; you can establish your profile and disambiguate your works from your name doppelgangers.

Researcher networks

  • Researchgate
  • Academia.edu

Be careful: these entice you to upload your papers, at your legal responsibility.
